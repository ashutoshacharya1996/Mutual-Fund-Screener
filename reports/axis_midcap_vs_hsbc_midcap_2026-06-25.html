💬 PLAIN ENGLISH — WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU

PWE (Probability-Weighted Expected Return) is just a realistic average return that accounts for all three scenarios rather than assuming the best case. We multiply each scenario's return by its probability and add them up.

Axis PWE = 9.39% · HSBC PWE = 8.53%. Axis wins. Yes, HSBC has a higher best-case return in Scenario A (18.2% vs 16.9%) — but it also has a much worse worst-case (-4.8% vs -1.9%). HSBC is a higher-variance bet. For your FIRE goal, consistent compounding beats boom-bust.

The "dispersion" numbers (18.8 vs 23.0) are like a turbulence score on a flight. Axis is a smoother ride. HSBC has wider swings. Both land, but you'll lose less sleep with Axis.

Recency Bias Detected
HSBC's 5Y return of 18.66% vs Axis lagging in recent periods is driving the switch impulse. Cheenu Gupta's tenure started Nov 2022 — the entire track record is a pure bull-market run. Shiny Object Syndrome
The capex / electrical equipment / capital markets theme running in HSBC's portfolio is currently hot (Scenario A tailwind). But this concentration — 13.38% electrical equipment + 12.5% capital markets — means HSBC is effectively a thematic fund wearing a mid cap label. Axis is more diversified across quality businesses and would outperform materially in Scenarios B or C.
⚠
Action Bias
The SIP mandate is clear: SIPs never stop. Switching creates LTCG friction (~1.5%), resets cost basis, and adds execution risk. Given both funds are HOLD-rated, inaction is the correct action. Doing nothing is a decision — and in this case, the right one.
